Imaging — When and What
Ultrasound (USS) — First Line on Admission
  • ✅All patients with AP should have USS on admission — look for gallstones, common bile duct (CBD) dilation, biliary sludge
  • ✅USS may show enlarged, hypoechoic pancreas — but often limited by overlying bowel gas
  • ✅CBD >8mm (or >10mm post-cholecystectomy) suggests obstruction → consider MRCP or ERCP
CT with IV Contrast — NOT Routine on Day 1
  • ✅Diagnostic uncertainty — cannot confirm AP by clinical + enzyme criteria
  • ✅Severe or deteriorating at 48–72h — assess for necrosis, complications (CTSI scoring)
  • ✅New fever or clinical deterioration — rule out infected necrosis, new collections, abscesses
  • ✅At 4 weeks — assess for walled-off necrosis (WON) before intervention planning
  • 🚫Mild AP with clear diagnosis — CT not needed. No management change and unnecessary radiation + contrast risk.
MRCP / EUS — When Gallstones Suspected but USS Unclear
  • ✅MRCP — non-invasive, no radiation, best for CBD stones, pancreatic duct anatomy, divisum
  • ✅EUS — if MRCP unavailable or inconclusive, or pre-ERCP assessment
  • ✅MRI abdomen with MRCP — useful if CT contraindicated (contrast allergy, renal impairment) or for soft tissue characterisation of necrosis

Special Situations
Hypertriglyceridaemia-induced AP (TG >11.3 mmol/L)
  • 01Insulin infusion 0.1–0.3 units/kg/hr — activates lipoprotein lipase, clears TG rapidly. Monitor glucose hourly.
  • 02Plasmapheresis if TG >22.6 mmol/L or rapid deterioration — removes TG directly. Specialist involvement essential.
  • 03Avoid glucose-containing fluids initially — may worsen hypertriglyceridaemia
  • 04Long-term: fenofibrate + dietary counselling after recovery

Pain Management — Stepwise
Adequate analgesia is essential — undertreated pain worsens stress response and ileus. Latest evidence supports opioids as safe in AP.
  • 01Paracetamol (Acetaminophen) — First Line
    1g IV/PO every 6–8h. Regular dosing, not PRN. Safe, effective, opioid-sparing. Reduce dose if liver disease.
  • 02NSAIDs — Use with Caution
    Diclofenac 75mg IM or rectal / Indomethacin 25–50mg TDS. Anti-inflammatory benefit. Avoid if AKI, renal impairment, peptic ulcer, or haemodynamic instability.
  • 03Tramadol — Moderate Pain
    50–100mg IV/PO every 6–8h. Weak opioid + serotonin/NE reuptake inhibitor. Avoid in seizure history. Nausea common — add antiemetic.
  • 04Morphine / Fentanyl — Severe Pain
    Morphine 2.5–5mg IV PRN or PCA. Fentanyl preferred if renal failure (no active metabolite accumulation). No evidence morphine worsens AP — old Oddi spasm concern is not supported by RCTs.
  • 05Epidural Analgesia — Severe / Refractory
    Thoracic epidural (T6–T10) in severe AP with intractable pain. Reduces splanchnic vasoconstriction, may improve pancreatic perfusion. Requires anaesthesia/pain team.
  • 06Antiemetics — Always Co-prescribe
    Ondansetron 4–8mg IV TDS or Metoclopramide 10mg TDS. Nausea worsens pain experience and prevents oral nutrition.
⚠️Avoid pethidine (meperidine) — neurotoxic metabolite norpethidine accumulates in renal failure → seizures. Not recommended in AP.
💊Epidural use early in severe AP is associated with reduced mortality and organ failure in observational studies. Consider in any severe AP with inadequate pain control on systemic opioids.

Antibiotic Guidelines
NO prophylactic antibiotics in AP — only treat confirmed or strongly suspected infection (ACG 2024, IAP/APA)
Indications for Antibiotics
  • ✅Infected pancreatic necrosis — CT: gas in necrosis, or FNA culture positive
  • ✅Acute cholangitis — Charcot's triad / Reynolds pentad (gallstone AP)
  • ✅Bacteraemia / sepsis — positive blood cultures or septic shock
  • ✅Concurrent infection — pneumonia, UTI, line infection
  • ✅PCT >1 + clinical deterioration + no other source — empirical antibiotics reasonable
  • 🚫Sterile necrosis alone — NO antibiotics. Prophylactic antibiotics do NOT prevent infected necrosis (IAP meta-analysis)

Culture Guidelines
  • 01Blood cultures × 2 sets — before antibiotics if fever ≥38.5°C or sepsis suspected. Repeat if fever spikes.
  • 02CT-guided FNA of necrosis — if infected necrosis suspected and no gas visible on CT. Gram stain + culture. However, FNA has 20% false-negative rate — clinical context matters.
  • 03Drain fluid culture — send sample from any pigtail drain insertion. Change antibiotics per sensitivities.
  • 04Urine culture — if catheterised and new fever. UTI common in ICU patients.
  • 05Duration — infected necrosis: 4–6 weeks total (IV then step down to oral). Review at 2 weeks with clinical/CRP/PCT response.
ERCP — Indications & Timing
ERCP is NOT routine in AP — only when biliary obstruction with cholangitis is confirmed
  • ✅Acute cholangitis — ERCP within 24 hours. Charcot's triad: RUQ pain + fever + jaundice.
  • ✅Persistent CBD obstruction without cholangitis — ERCP within 72h if bilirubin not improving
  • 🚫Predicted severe AP without cholangitis — NO benefit shown. ERCP does not prevent complications in this group.
  • 🚫Mild gallstone AP with spontaneous passage — ERCP not needed. Cholecystectomy planned instead.
🫶Rectal indomethacin 100mg given at time of ERCP reduces post-ERCP pancreatitis risk. Recommend for all patients undergoing ERCP.
Pancreatic Necrosis — Step-Up Approach
PANTER & TENSION trials — minimally invasive step-up is superior to open surgery
  • 01Conservative first — sterile necrosis: antibiotics NOT needed. Wait and watch. Many resolve without intervention.
  • 02Delay intervention 4+ weeks — allow walled-off necrosis (WON) to form. Liquefied WON is far easier to drain than early acute necrotic collection. Only deviate if septic shock or deteriorating.
  • 03Step 1: Percutaneous / Endoscopic Drainage (Pigtail) — first intervention. CT/US-guided pigtail drain into collection. Can also use endoscopic ultrasound-guided transmural drainage (EUCD) if close to stomach/duodenum wall.
  • 04Step 2: Minimally Invasive Necrosectomy — if drainage inadequate. Video-assisted retroperitoneal debridement (VARD) or endoscopic necrosectomy via stent. Preferred over open.
  • 05Step 3: Open Surgical Necrosectomy — last resort only. High morbidity/mortality. Reserved for failed minimally invasive approach, bowel perforation, uncontrolled haemorrhage.

Cholecystectomy Timing
  • ✅Mild gallstone AP — same admission cholecystectomy before discharge. Reduces recurrent AP risk significantly (PONCHO trial). Ideally within 48–72h of symptom resolution.
  • ⏳Moderately severe / Severe AP — delay cholecystectomy until full recovery, local complications resolved, and patient fit for surgery. Typically 6–8 weeks.
  • ⏳Necrosis present — defer until necrosis managed and patient optimised. Concurrent cholecystectomy at time of necrosectomy may be considered by experienced surgeons.

Things Commonly Missed in AP
  • ⚠️Etiology not identified — if gallstones excluded, check TG, calcium, medications, IgG4 (autoimmune), anatomical (MRCP for divisum)
  • ⚠️Fluid overload — continuing aggressive IVF beyond 48h in a resuscitated patient. Causes ARDS, ACS, worsens outcomes.
  • ⚠️Bowel rest ordered unnecessarily — early oral feeding is evidence-based. NBM increases complications.
  • ⚠️Prophylactic antibiotics prescribed — not indicated in sterile necrosis. Drives resistance.
  • ⚠️DVT prophylaxis omitted — start LMWH when haemostasis confirmed, usually 24–48h. AP patients at high VTE risk.
  • ⚠️Stress ulcer prophylaxis forgotten — PPI in all ICU AP patients. Risk of GI bleeding especially with coagulopathy.
  • ⚠️Same-admission cholecystectomy missed — for mild gallstone AP. Reduces risk of recurrence from 18% to 1.5% (PONCHO trial).
  • ⚠️Alcohol counselling not done — if alcohol aetiology, refer to addiction services before discharge.
  • ⚠️Pseudoaneurysm missed — unexplained anaemia drop + abdominal pain in a patient with necrosis = urgent CT angio.
  • ⚠️Glycaemic control neglected — AP causes insulin resistance. Target 6–10 mmol/L. New-onset DM may develop post-AP.
